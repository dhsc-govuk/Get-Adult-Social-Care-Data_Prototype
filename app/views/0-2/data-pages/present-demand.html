Current demand

The number of care beds per 100,000 people allows you to compare care provision between different areas. A higher number may suggest greater demand, particularly when occupancy rates are high.

Demand can also be shaped by population need. Learn about drivers of demand.

Care homes in Suffolk have 1,024 beds per 100,000 people - 31.6% more than the East of England average (778).

Despite this higher provision, Suffolk care homes are operating at 82% occupancy, indicating that capacity may still be constrained.

In comparison, the East of England's 78% occupancy rate suggests some areas have slightly more capacity to meet demand.

Drivers of demand

Demand for adult social care is influenced by a range of factors, including the age and disability profile of your population. Areas with a higher proportion of older adults or people with disabilities typically experience greater pressure on services.

Suffolk has a larger proportion of people aged 65+ (18%) compared to the national average (7%). This suggests demand for care services in this local authority would be higher than average.

Summary of key trends

The dataset reveals differences in bed availability and occupancy rates for adult social care across different regions in England. Suffolk has a higher number of beds per 100,000 people (1,024) compared to the East of England (778). Additionally, bed occupancy rates differ significantly by age, with Suffolk showing higher occupancy rates for both age groups (35% for ages 18-65 and 75% for ages 65 and over) compared to the East of England (39% for ages 18-65 and 61% for ages 65 and over).

Insight 1: Capacity utilisation in Suffolk versus the East of England

Suffolk has a higher number of beds per 100,000 people (1,024) compared to the East of England (778). Despite this higher capacity, both regions have high bed occupancy rates, with Suffolk at 82% and the East of England at 78%. This indicates a strong demand for adult social care services in both regions.

How you might use this insight

  • Consider increasing bed capacity in regions like Suffolk and the East of England, where occupancy rates are high despite having a relatively higher number of beds. This could involve investing in new facilities or expanding existing ones.
  • Evaluate the efficiency and outcomes of current services in these regions to ensure that the high demand is being met effectively, potentially by reallocating resources or improving care management practices.

Insight 2: Age-related bed occupancy trends

The data shows that the older population (65 and over) represents a larger proportion of bed occupancy in both Suffolk (75%) and the East of England (61%) compared to the younger group (18-65). This suggests that the demand for beds is driven more by the older population.

How you might use this insight

  • Focus on tailored care programs and facilities specifically catering to the older population, ensuring that these programs meet the unique needs of this demographic.
  • Develop preventive care initiatives aimed at reducing the need for bed occupancy among older adults, such as enhanced community care services and support for independent living.

Beds per 100,000 people
    Details for Beds per 100,000 people
    What this measures The total number of beds recorded by care providers across health and adult social care, adjusted to a rate of 100,000 people in the population.
    Data source Capacity Tracker (opens in new tab)
    Data last updated 5 February 2025
    Update frequency Daily
    Date range From 30 September 2017 to 5 February 2025.
    Methodology The total number of beds is the number of beds reported by care providers, including those in use and those available for new admissions.

    Total beds includes mental health, learning disabilities, and end-of-life care beds in residential and nursing care homes.The data covers both self-funded and local authority-funded beds.

    Care providers update this information daily or whenever changes occur using the Capacity Tracker tool. They report how many beds are available and occupied in care settings such as nursing homes, residential care, and community care.

    The data is grouped by local areas, regions, and nationally so it is easier to compare. The information is checked and updated regularly to make sure it is as accurate as possible.
